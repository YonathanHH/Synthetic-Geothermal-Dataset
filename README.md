## Overview

This dataset provides a large-scale synthetic representation of geothermal wells worldwide, designed for use in data science workflows, machine learning model development, geothermal reservoir analysis, and energy transition research. Every record is generated using research-grade geophysical models, ensuring that physical relationships between depth, temperature, pressure, porosity, and permeability are internally consistent and grounded in real-world geothermal science.

The dataset covers three reservoir archetypes: Vapor-Dominated, Liquid-Dominated, and Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) and spanning major producing countries including Indonesia, USA, Philippines, Kenya, Iceland, Italy, New Zealand, Turkey, Japan, and Mexico.

## Well ID Convention

Well IDs follow the format:

```
[FIELDCODE]_W[####]       → Production well  (e.g., KAMOJANG_W0042)
[FIELDCODE]_W[####]I      → Injection well   (e.g., KAMOJANG_W0042I)
```

- `FIELDCODE` is derived from the field name: spaces and hyphens removed, uppercased, truncated to 8 characters
- `####` is a zero-padded sequential integer (4 digits) unique within each field and well type
- All 20,000 well IDs are globally unique across the entire dataset

## Physics & Generation Methodology

### Philosophy

Rather than sampling purely from statistical distributions, each well's properties are generated through a cascade of physics-informed relationships. Field-type determines the pressure regime; depth drives temperature through a gradient model; porosity decreases with depth; and permeability is derived analytically from porosity rather than sampled independently.

### Temperature Model

Bottomhole temperature is computed as a blend of a depth-gradient model and a field-level anchor:

\[ T_{well} = 0.7 \cdot (T_{surface} + \Gamma \cdot z) + 0.3 \cdot T_{field\_mean} + \epsilon \]

where \(T_{surface} \sim \mathcal{U}(10, 25)\) °C, \(\Gamma\) is the field-type-specific geothermal gradient, \(z\) is the well depth in metres, and \(\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.3\sigma_{field})\) is random noise. Geothermal gradients are drawn from:

- **Vapor-Dominated:** \(\Gamma \sim \mathcal{U}(80, 120)\) °C/km
- **Liquid-Dominated:** \(\Gamma \sim \mathcal{U}(60, 100)\) °C/km
- **EGS:** \(\Gamma \sim \mathcal{U}(40, 70)\) °C/km

These ranges reflect the higher heat flux found in vapor-dominated systems (e.g., The Geysers, Kamojang) versus the lower gradients typical of basement-hosted EGS resources like Soultz-sous-Forêts and Cooper Basin.

### Pressure Model

Three distinct pressure regimes are applied based on field type:

**Liquid-Dominated (hydrostatic):**

\[ P = \rho_{brine} \cdot g \cdot z \]

with \(\rho_{brine} = 900\) kg/m³ (saline brine, slightly denser than freshwater).

**Vapor-Dominated (steam-static):**

\[ P = \rho_{steam} \cdot g \cdot z + P_{0} \]

with \(\rho_{steam} \approx 8\) kg/m³ and \(P_0 = 0.1\) MPa surface offset. This yields the characteristically gentle pressure gradients (2–6 MPa) observed in steam-cap systems such as Larderello and The Geysers.

**EGS (lithostatic fraction):**

\[ P = 0.60 \cdot \rho_{rock} \cdot g \cdot z \]

with \(\rho_{rock} = 2700\) kg/m³. The 0.60 factor reflects that EGS pore pressures are intermediate between hydrostatic and full lithostatic, consistent with stimulated hard-rock reservoirs at 3–6 km depth.

All pressure values are clipped to physically plausible bounds of 0.5–50.0 MPa.

### Porosity Model

Porosity is anchored to the field-level mean and penalised with depth:

\[ \phi = \phi_{field} + \mathcal{N}(0, 0.18\phi_{field}) - 0.008 \cdot \Delta z_{km} \]

where \(\Delta z_{km}\) is the deviation from the field mean depth in kilometres. This captures the well-established compaction and cementation trend in geothermal reservoirs. Values are clipped to [0.01, 0.25].

### Permeability Model (Kozeny-Carman)

Permeability is derived analytically from porosity using the **Kozeny-Carman equation**:

\[ k = \frac{\phi^3 \cdot d^2}{180 \cdot \tau \cdot (1 - \phi)^2} \]

where \(d\) is the characteristic grain diameter (1×10⁻⁴ m), \(\tau = 2.5\) is tortuosity, and \(k\) is in m², then converted to milliDarcy (1 mD = 9.869×10⁻¹⁶ m²). This analytical baseline is then scaled by a field-level multiplier calibrated to the known average permeability of each field, and a log-normal multiplicative noise term is applied:

\[ k_{well} = k_{KC} \cdot k_{scale} \cdot e^{\mathcal{N}(0, 0.6)} \]

The log-normal scatter reflects the well-known log-normal distribution of permeability in natural rock formations. Final values are clipped to [0.01, 5000] mD.

### Flow Rate Model

Production flow rates are sampled from a truncated normal distribution whose mean is permeability- and temperature-informed:

\[ \dot{m}_{mean} = 25 + 0.05k + 0.03(T - 150) \]

where \(k\) is permeability in mD and \(T\) is temperature in °C. This encodes the physical intuition that higher-permeability, hotter wells tend to be more productive. Flow rates are clipped to [2, 120] kg/s.

Injection well flow rates follow a similar distribution (mean ~18 kg/s), then are negated to be negative, as per the dataset convention.

### Power Generation Model

The electrical power output of each production well is estimated using the empirical thermal-to-electric model:

\[ P_{MWe} = \eta \cdot \dot{m} \cdot C_p \cdot (T - T_{ref}) \times 10^{-3} \]

where \(\eta = 0.12\) (12% thermal-to-electric conversion efficiency, typical for single-flash and dry-steam cycles), \(C_p = 4.18\) kJ/(kg·K), and \(T_{ref} = 15\) °C. Injection wells are assigned `power_generated_MWe = 0.0`.

## Reservoir Type Characteristics

### Vapor-Dominated Systems

Vapor-dominated reservoirs occur where steam is the continuous pressure-controlling phase. Examples in this dataset include The Geysers (USA), Kamojang, Darajat, and Wayang Windu (Indonesia), Larderello and Monte Amiata (Italy), and Matsukawa (Japan).

Typical characteristics in the dataset:
- **Depth:** 1,500–3,500 m
- **Temperature:** 200–290°C
- **Pressure:** 1–6 MPa (steam-static gradient)
- **Porosity:** 0.03–0.07
- **Permeability:** 5–25 mD

### Liquid-Dominated Systems

Liquid-dominated reservoirs are the most common globally, where liquid water is the dominant phase. This category includes the majority of Indonesian, Philippine, Kenyan, Icelandic, and Turkish fields in the dataset.

Typical characteristics in the dataset:
- **Depth:** 1,000–4,000 m
- **Temperature:** 150–380°C
- **Pressure:** 8–30 MPa (hydrostatic)
- **Porosity:** 0.06–0.15
- **Permeability:** 15–200 mD

### Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS)

EGS fields exploit heat from low-permeability basement rocks by hydraulic stimulation. Examples include Soultz-sous-Forêts (France), Insheim and Landau (Germany), Desert Peak (USA), Newberry Volcano (USA), and Cooper Basin (Australia).

Typical characteristics in the dataset:
- **Depth:** 2,500–6,000 m
- **Temperature:** 150–300°C
- **Pressure:** 20–45 MPa (lithostatic fraction)
- **Porosity:** 0.01–0.06
- **Permeability:** 1–15 mD
- **Flow rate:** 2–30 kg/s (lower than conventional wells)

## Limitations & Synthetic Data Caveats

This dataset is **synthetic** and should not be used as a substitute for real measured well data in engineering design or regulatory submissions. The following simplifications apply:

1. **Temperature-depth relationship** uses a single-value geothermal gradient per field type rather than a full numerical conduction-convection model. Real systems can exhibit thermal inversions, convective upwellings, and lateral heterogeneity not captured here.

2. **Kozeny-Carman permeability** assumes a granular porous medium. Many geothermal reservoirs are fracture-dominated (especially EGS and volcanic systems), where permeability is controlled by fracture aperture and connectivity rather than matrix grain size.

3. **Two-phase effects** (steam fraction, flash processes) are not explicitly modelled. In real vapor-dominated and two-phase systems, enthalpy rather than temperature alone governs power output.

4. **Temporal dynamics** are absent. Real geothermal fields show pressure drawdown, temperature decline, scaling, and recharge over time. This dataset represents a single static snapshot.

5. **Well interference** between adjacent production and injection wells is not modelled. In real fields, well spacing and interference strongly influence individual well deliverability.

6. **Geographic coordinates** are jittered from field centroids with Gaussian noise (~5 km standard deviation) and should not be used for precise spatial analysis.

## Quick Start (Python)

```python
import pandas as pd

# Load main dataset
df = pd.read_csv("geothermal_wells_worldwide.csv")

# Load field summary
summary = pd.read_csv("geothermal_fields_summary.csv")

# Filter production wells only
prod = df[df["well_type"] == "Production"]

# Mean temperature by field type
print(df.groupby("field_type")["temperature_C"].mean())

# Top 10 fields by total power output
top_fields = summary.sort_values("total_power_MWe", ascending=False).head(10)
print(top_fields[["field_name", "country", "field_type", "total_power_MWe"]])

# Correlation matrix for reservoir properties
reservoir_cols = ["well_depth_m", "temperature_C", "pressure_MPa",
                  "porosity", "permeability_mD", "flow_rate_kg_s", "power_generated_MWe"]
print(prod[reservoir_cols].corr().round(3))
```
